# Dancing with the Gods: Aegean Dawn

Alternative Comprehensive Game Design Document

Version: 0.1  
Platform: Mobile, iOS and Android  
Orientation: Portrait  
Genre: Simulation and Management / Persistent RPG Card Game / Mythic Dance Ritual / Town Restoration  
Core Theme: A mortal dancer saves her town by earning divine favor through ritual dance, civic management, and relationships with the gods.

---

## 1. Design Intent

This document explores a second theme direction for the dance deckbuilder: a Greek mythology version where a mortal protagonist restores a dying town by dancing for, with, and sometimes against the gods.

This is an alternative concept, not a replacement for the gothic vampire version. The goal is to test whether the core structure can support a broader mythic management fantasy:

```text
Manage town by day -> perform sacred dances by night -> earn divine favor -> restore civic life -> face greater divine demands
```

The central shift is from intimate gothic manor restoration to public town survival. The player is no longer primarily restoring a private estate and deepening one central romance. She is managing a community, balancing multiple gods, preparing festivals, collecting choreography cards, and deciding what kind of future her town deserves.

### Core Premise

The coastal town of Heliara is dying. The springs have slowed, the harvest has failed, ships avoid the harbor, and the gods no longer answer ordinary prayers. The protagonist, a mortal dancer trained in sacred rites, discovers that the town's ancient covenant with Olympus has been broken.

Only dance can carry a mortal plea strongly enough to reach the gods.

Each day, the player manages Heliara's resources, repairs civic spaces, prepares offerings, trains the chorus, and chooses which divine patron to invoke. Each night, she performs a card-driven ritual dance with a god, a divine avatar, or a chorus of townspeople. Her performance earns Favor, Hope, resources, and blessings that visibly restore the town.

### Design Thesis

The Greek mythology theme is strongest if it leans into:

- Multiple divine patrons with distinct play styles.
- Town simulation and civic survival.
- Festival planning and audience management.
- Public ritual spectacle.
- Moral choices about divine power, mortal agency, and civic responsibility.

It is weakest if it becomes a generic "date every god" game with no town-management spine. The gods should be attractive, powerful, dramatic, and emotionally charged, but the heart of the game should be the mortal protagonist saving her people.

---

## 2. Canon Rules

These rules define the alternative design.

- The player is a mortal dancer, not a demigod combat hero.
- The player owns a persistent choreography collection.
- The standard active ritual deck is 20 cards once fully unlocked.
- Tutorial days use smaller guided decks.
- Each night is a sacred performance, not a roguelike run.
- Failure causes reduced Favor, lost Composure, damaged town morale, angered patrons, or a shortened rite.
- There is no permadeath.
- The town persists and visibly changes over time.
- Buildings, god relationships, cards, outfits, chorus training, and civic stats carry forward.
- The player manages both divine favor and mortal needs.
- The gods are not interchangeable romance skins. Each god has a domain, agenda, performance style, and management consequence.
- Apollo is the recommended first divine partner because music, healing, prophecy, and light naturally fit the dance premise.

## 3. Executive Summary

### Working Title

**Dancing with the Gods: Aegean Dawn**

### Logline

A mythic town-management dance deckbuilder where a mortal performer saves her coastal town by preparing festivals, restoring temples, and performing sacred card-driven dances with Apollo, Hades, Aphrodite, Zeus, and the Olympian court.

### High Concept

Heliara was once a beloved town of music, harvest, and sea trade. Now its temples are cracked, its wells are dry, and its people are losing hope. When the protagonist discovers an ancient dance altar beneath the ruined theatre, Apollo answers her first rite and reveals the truth: the town's covenant with the gods has fractured.

To save Heliara, the player must rebuild civic life and renew the town's relationship with the divine. By day, she manages resources, repairs buildings, trains dancers, gathers offerings, accepts commissions, and chooses which god to petition. By night, she performs sacred dances built from choreography cards. Every successful performance earns divine Favor that can heal the town, unlock new gods, upgrade cards, and change the course of the story.

The game combines:

- Persistent deck construction.
- Ritual dance tactics.
- Town restoration.
- Festival and audience management.
- Divine relationship routes.
- Resource planning.
- Visible civic transformation.

### Target Audience

- Cozy simulation players who enjoy restoring towns, managing resources, and seeing visible progress.
- Romance and narrative players interested in mythic characters and emotionally charged divine encounters.
- Players who enjoy Greek mythology, gods, prophecies, curses, and mythic drama.
- Mobile players who want short daily loops with meaningful long-term progression.
- Deck-construction players who enjoy optimizing combos without roguelike resets.

### Pitch Positioning

The game fits Simulation and Management by making the town the persistent center of play. Dance performances are the exciting nightly source of power, but the player's long-term success depends on planning festivals, balancing gods, repairing civic buildings, allocating resources, and deciding which citizens to help first.

The core pitch:

```text
You are not fighting the gods.
You are dancing well enough that they must listen.
```

## 4. Design Pillars

### Pillar 1: Mortal Agency Against Divine Power

The protagonist is mortal, but she is not passive. She negotiates with gods through beauty, discipline, ritual knowledge, and civic leadership. The player should feel small before Olympus, but never powerless.

### Pillar 2: Town Survival Is the Main Management Layer

Heliara must feel like a living place. Citizens get hungry, festivals raise morale, trade routes reopen, temples regain color, and poor planning has consequences. Divine favor matters because it changes daily life.

### Pillar 3: Dance as Sacred Diplomacy

Dance is not just performance. It is prayer, treaty, challenge, seduction, mourning, healing, and public leadership. Each god reads the dance differently.

### Pillar 4: Many Gods, Many Strategies

Each divine patron should change how the player builds decks and manages the town. Apollo rewards rhythm and healing. Athena rewards planning. Dionysus rewards risk. Hades rewards restraint and memory. Aphrodite rewards emotional expression and audience connection.

### Pillar 5: Festivals Make Management Visible

The town should gather to watch the player perform. Better buildings, trained chorus members, restored lights, prepared offerings, and invited guests should all appear in the performance space.

### Pillar 6: Persistent Progress, No Run Resets

This is not a roguelike. Cards, buildings, gods, outfits, civic stats, and story choices persist. A failed rite can hurt the town's short-term state, but it does not erase progress.

### Pillar 7: Mythic Spectacle With Cozy Readability

The visual tone should be luminous, elegant, and emotionally readable. This is not grim historical simulation. It is a playable myth with marble, sunlight, olive groves, storm clouds, gold leaf, torchlight, sea foam, flowers, shadows, and divine transformations.

## 5. Core Game Structure

The game uses a repeating calendar with day, dusk, night, and dawn phases.

```text
Day: Manage town and prepare
Dusk: Choose rite, patron, venue, offerings, and deck
Night: Perform sacred dance
Dawn: Resolve blessings, town changes, rewards, and story
```

### 5.1 Day: Govern and Prepare

Each day gives the player **3 Civic Action slots**.

Choose 3 of 7:

- **Rehearse choreography:** Merge duplicates, refine the active deck, train a specific combo, or improve chorus timing.
- **Repair a civic site:** Spend Favor, stone, wood, coin, or labor to restore part of Heliara.
- **Prepare offerings:** Gather laurel, honey, wine, flowers, incense, grain, shells, or votive statues for a specific god.
- **Help citizens:** Resolve a town request, improve Hope, reduce unrest, heal sickness, or gain local reputation.
- **Consult a god:** Spend Favor or visit a shrine for dialogue, prophecy, a boon, or a risk warning.
- **Trade at the agora:** Buy materials, sell surplus goods, set festival donation tiers, or accept commissions.
- **Scout beyond town:** Send citizens to groves, cliffs, ruins, farms, sea caves, or the necropolis for resources and card duplicates.

Free supporting actions:

- Inspect town map.
- Review the Choreia Codex.
- Change outfits.
- Read unlocked god lore.
- Preview tonight's divine cues.
- Check citizen requests.
- Compare building upgrade paths.

### 5.2 Dusk: Plan the Rite

Dusk is the management handoff into performance.

The player chooses:

- Divine patron to invoke.
- Performance venue.
- Active deck.
- Outfit and accessories.
- Offering bundle.
- Festival scale.
- Audience policy.
- Chorus assignment.
- Risk level.

Example choices:

| Choice | Low Risk | High Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Festival Scale | Private shrine rite | Full town festival |
| Audience | Citizens only | Citizens plus foreign patrons |
| Offering | Standard laurel and honey | Rare relic or expensive sacrifice |
| Patron | Apollo, known cues | Zeus, volatile storm cues |
| Chorus | Small trained chorus | Large untrained crowd |

### 5.3 Night: Perform and Petition

At night, the player performs a sacred dance.

Night actions include:

- Draw cards from the active deck.
- Build exact 8-step ritual phrases.
- Read the invoked god's divine cue.
- Match stance and gesture requirements.
- Generate and spend Grace.
- Build Resolve to withstand difficult divine demands.
- Maintain Composure.
- Trigger cinematic dance replays.
- Earn Favor, Hope, Coin, resources, and story progress.

### 5.4 Dawn: Blessings and Consequences

At dawn, the rite resolves.

Dawn outcomes include:

- Apply divine blessing to town stats.
- Spend Favor on building restoration.
- Unlock cards, gods, venues, or civic systems.
- Resolve patron mood.
- Resolve citizen satisfaction.
- Bank relationship progress.
- Update town crisis tracks.
- Preview tomorrow's requests, shortages, and opportunities.

The dawn phase should show that the performance mattered. A dry fountain starts running. Fishermen return with full nets. A sick child wakes. Olive leaves turn silver in the sun. A temple statue opens its eyes.

## 6. The Town of Heliara

### 6.1 Town Identity

Heliara is a fictional coastal town in a mythic Aegean setting. It is built around a harbor, an old theatre, olive terraces, temples, a market street, and a spring-fed plaza.

Heliara should feel small enough that the player knows its people, but large enough to support meaningful civic management.

Core locations:

- Temple Hill.
- Ruined Theatre.
- Agora.
- Harbor.
- Olive Grove.
- Granary.
- House of Healing.
- Artisan Quarter.
- Fountain Plaza.
- Necropolis Road.
- Cliff Shrine.
- Festival Grounds.

### 6.2 Town Crisis

The town suffers from a supernatural decline called **The Waning**.

Symptoms:

- Springs produce less water.
- Crops fail.
- Trade ships avoid the harbor.
- Citizens dream of silent gods.
- Temple flames burn blue and weak.
- Children stop singing old festival songs.
- Marble statues crack at dawn.

The Waning is not only a curse. It is a broken relationship between mortals, gods, ancestors, and the land. This gives the story more depth than "appease gods to win."

### 6.3 Town Stats

The player manages several persistent civic stats.

| Stat | Meaning | Low-State Consequence | High-State Benefit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hope | Citizen morale and belief in the town's future | More unrest, weaker chorus, lower festival attendance | Bigger audience, bonus Favor conversion |
| Food | Stored grain, olives, fish, and livestock | Citizens go hungry, sickness rises | Better offerings, stable population |
| Health | Physical and spiritual wellbeing | Lost action efficiency, urgent requests | More labor, better festival stamina |
| Safety | Protection from storms, bandits, beasts, and divine backlash | Resource loss, citizen fear | More scouting options |
| Piety | Civic trust in rituals and gods | Gods require stronger offerings | Cheaper invocations, better cue previews |
| Culture | Music, dance, art, memory, and education | Weak card rewards, dull festivals | Stronger cards, better audience rewards |
| Coin | Local economy and trade | Limited repairs and purchases | Market access, imported offerings |

### 6.4 Crisis Tracks

Instead of a single fail timer, Heliara has crisis tracks that rise and fall.

Suggested tracks:

- **Drought:** tied to Demeter, Poseidon, Apollo, and town infrastructure.
- **Unrest:** tied to Hope, Coin, Hera, Athena, and public festivals.
- **Sickness:** tied to Apollo, Hades, clean water, and House of Healing.
- **Storm Season:** tied to Zeus, Poseidon, harbor repairs, and safety.
- **Memory Loss:** tied to Hades, Persephone, Culture, and the Necropolis.

If a crisis track fills, the town suffers a setback:

- Building damage.
- Lost resources.
- Citizens unavailable.
- Higher offering costs.
- Patron anger.
- Story complication.

The game should avoid hard failure unless the player chooses a high-stakes challenge. The normal consequence is pressure, not a full reset.

## 7. Divine Patrons

Each god has:

- Domain.
- Personality.
- Dance identity.
- Divine cue patterns.
- Town-management benefits.
- Relationship track.
- Risk if neglected or angered.
- Signature card pool.

### 7.1 Apollo

**Domain:** Music, sunlight, prophecy, healing, order.  
**Role:** First patron and tutorial guide.  
**Dance Style:** Clear rhythm, symmetrical phrases, luminous gestures, exact timing.  
**Town Benefit:** Healing, Culture, prophecy previews, improved cue clarity.  
**Risk:** Overemphasis on perfection can reduce improvisation rewards.

Apollo teaches the player that dance can be sacred communication. He should be charismatic, precise, warm at first glance, and more demanding as his route deepens.

Signature mechanics:

- Better lead preview.
- Bonus for exact 8-step phrases.
- Healing blessings after no-Stumble performances.
- Prophecy cards that preview the next draw or divine cue.

Example cue:

```text
Golden Cadence
Timing: Steps 5-8
Response: Play a Gesture attached to a Step card
Success: +Apollo Favor and +Health
Miss: Reduced Favor, no penalty
```

### 7.2 Artemis

**Domain:** Hunt, wilderness, moonlight, independence, protection.  
**Role:** Protector of the outskirts and scouting systems.  
**Dance Style:** Sharp footwork, evasive turns, moonlit solo phrases.  
**Town Benefit:** Safety, wilderness resources, protection from beasts.  
**Risk:** Dislikes exploitative festivals and excessive courtly display.

Signature mechanics:

- Solo stance bonuses.
- Scout success boosts.
- Protection from crisis events.
- Cards that convert unused Grace into Safety.

### 7.3 Aphrodite

**Domain:** Love, beauty, desire, social harmony, attraction.  
**Role:** Audience, guest, romance, and social reputation patron.  
**Dance Style:** Expressive gestures, close partner moments, crowd connection.  
**Town Benefit:** Hope, festival attendance, diplomacy, marriage alliances.  
**Risk:** Shallow spectacle can backfire if the town's real needs are ignored.

Signature mechanics:

- Audience reaction bonuses.
- Gesture card discounts.
- Relationship scenes.
- Guest requests and social commissions.

### 7.4 Athena

**Domain:** Strategy, crafts, civic order, wisdom, defense.  
**Role:** Management optimization and planning patron.  
**Dance Style:** Structured sequences, planned transitions, tactical card control.  
**Town Benefit:** Efficient repairs, better labor assignment, defense, craft upgrades.  
**Risk:** Low tolerance for wasteful action planning.

Signature mechanics:

- Redraws and card ordering.
- Reduced repair costs.
- Civic edict upgrades.
- Bonus for matching planned phrase objectives.

### 7.5 Demeter

**Domain:** Harvest, grain, mothers, fertility, seasonal stability.  
**Role:** Food economy and family wellbeing patron.  
**Dance Style:** Grounded steps, circular chorus patterns, seasonal repetition.  
**Town Benefit:** Food, crop growth, family requests, winter preparation.  
**Risk:** Anger rises if the town spends lavishly while citizens are hungry.

Signature mechanics:

- Chorus stance bonuses.
- Food conversion into offerings.
- Harvest festivals.
- Cards that grow stronger over repeated performances.

### 7.6 Dionysus

**Domain:** Wine, theatre, ecstasy, masks, release, transformation.  
**Role:** Festival risk-reward and crowd excitement patron.  
**Dance Style:** Improvisation, sudden tempo shifts, chain multipliers.  
**Town Benefit:** Culture, Hope spikes, festival revenue, rare card drops.  
**Risk:** Unrest, lost Composure, or messy aftermath if pushed too far.

Signature mechanics:

- Risk multipliers.
- Random bonus cards.
- Audience frenzy.
- Mask cards that temporarily change card type or stance.

### 7.7 Hermes

**Domain:** Trade, travel, messages, luck, thieves, thresholds.  
**Role:** Market, errands, scouting, trade routes.  
**Dance Style:** Fast transitions, quick steps, handoff patterns.  
**Town Benefit:** Coin, trade goods, faster scouting, commission access.  
**Risk:** Volatile deals and tempting shortcuts.

Signature mechanics:

- Draw and discard.
- Market price manipulation.
- Trade-route unlocks.
- Cards that reward playing different card types in sequence.

### 7.8 Hera

**Domain:** Queenship, vows, households, legitimacy, social order.  
**Role:** Alliances, civic law, households, long-term stability.  
**Dance Style:** Formal partner patterns, vow gestures, ceremonial precision.  
**Town Benefit:** Unrest reduction, family stability, alliance rewards.  
**Risk:** Disapproves of broken promises and chaotic patron juggling.

Signature mechanics:

- Vow cards that commit the player to an objective for a large reward.
- Household requests.
- Alliance festivals.
- Relationship-bound boons with obligations.

### 7.9 Poseidon

**Domain:** Sea, earthquakes, horses, storms, harbors.  
**Role:** Harbor, fishing, sea trade, weather risk.  
**Dance Style:** Sweeping movement, surges, wave-like rhythm.  
**Town Benefit:** Fish, trade ships, weather control, harbor repair.  
**Risk:** Storms, damage, and sudden tempo disruption.

Signature mechanics:

- Surge cues that demand strong Resolve.
- Big reward for maintaining Composure through disruption.
- Harbor income.
- Weather-modified performances.

### 7.10 Hades

**Domain:** Underworld, wealth beneath the earth, ancestors, oaths, memory.  
**Role:** Ancestor rites, memory restoration, buried resources, moral gravity.  
**Dance Style:** Stillness, restraint, shadowed partner movement, precise pauses.  
**Town Benefit:** Memory, stability, rare minerals, ancestor blessings.  
**Risk:** Poorly prepared rites can lower Hope or invite grief events.

Hades should not be treated as evil. He is solemn, exacting, and bound by law.

Signature mechanics:

- Pause cards.
- Underworld stance.
- Memory recovery.
- Converting loss into long-term power.

### 7.11 Persephone

**Domain:** Spring, underworld queenship, seasons, return, transformation.  
**Role:** Bridge between Demeter and Hades systems.  
**Dance Style:** Seasonal transitions, bloom and shadow, dual-state cards.  
**Town Benefit:** Seasonal balance, transformation, endings, renewal.  
**Risk:** Her route exposes hard truths about the Waning.

Signature mechanics:

- Cards with Spring and Shadow modes.
- Crisis-track conversion.
- Seasonal festivals.
- Rare reconciliation scenes between divine domains.

### 7.12 Zeus

**Domain:** Sky, thunder, kingship, law, oaths, divine authority.  
**Role:** High-risk Olympus patron and late early-game escalation.  
**Dance Style:** Powerful cues, storm timing, sudden authority checks.  
**Town Benefit:** Major Favor, storm control, political legitimacy, large blessings.  
**Risk:** Severe backlash if the player overpromises or fails public rites.

Zeus is powerful but should be handled carefully. He can be commanding, charismatic, and dangerous without making the game revolve around coercive romance. His core route should focus on authority, law, storms, and the mortal town's right to be heard.

Signature mechanics:

- Thunder cues.
- Oath checks.
- High Favor rewards.
- Crisis suppression with future obligations.

## 8. Relationship and Favor Systems

### 8.1 Favor

Favor is the main divine currency. Each god has a separate Favor track.

Favor sources:

- Successful dances.
- Correct offerings.
- Matching divine cues.
- Completing god-specific town requests.
- Restoring that god's shrine.
- Choosing dialogue responses aligned with that god's values.

Favor uses:

- Unlock god scenes.
- Purchase blessings.
- Unlock card pools.
- Restore god-linked buildings.
- Ask for prophecy or protection.
- Negotiate crisis relief.

### 8.2 Bond

Bond measures personal relationship with a god. It is separate from Favor.

Favor asks: "Will this god help the town?"  
Bond asks: "Does this god know and care about the protagonist?"

Bond can unlock:

- Private scenes.
- Unique dialogue.
- Signature cards.
- Alternate divine cues.
- Temporary performance boons.
- Route endings.

Not every god needs to be romanceable. Some can support mentor, rival, patron, family-like, or solemn alliance arcs.

### 8.3 Divine Mood

Each god has a short-term mood that affects the next invocation.

Mood examples:

- Pleased.
- Curious.
- Demanding.
- Neglected.
- Jealous.
- Wounded.
- Testing.

Mood is affected by:

- Recent performances.
- Offering quality.
- Keeping or breaking vows.
- Helping or ignoring citizens tied to that god.
- Invoking rival gods repeatedly.
- Festival choices.

### 8.4 Patron Balance

The player should not be punished for liking one god most, but the town should suffer if the player ignores entire domains.

Example:

- Ignoring Demeter can create food stress.
- Ignoring Hermes can slow trade.
- Ignoring Hades can worsen Memory Loss.
- Ignoring Athena can make repairs inefficient.

This creates management depth without forcing equal grinding.

## 9. Resources and Economy

### 9.1 Core Resources

| Resource | Source | Uses |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Favor | Night performances and god requests | Blessings, shrine upgrades, card unlocks |
| Hope | Festivals, citizen aid, visible restoration | Better attendance, stronger chorus, unrest control |
| Coin | Trade, donations, commissions | Building repairs, market goods, imported offerings |
| Food | Farms, fishing, Demeter blessings | Feed citizens, prepare offerings, stabilize town |
| Materials | Scouting, trade, repairs | Restore buildings and craft outfits |
| Offerings | Gathering, market, crafting | Invoke gods and improve rite rewards |
| Choreography Shards | Rewards, scouting, commissions | Merge and upgrade cards |
| Reputation | Successful public events | Attract patrons, pilgrims, teachers, traders |

### 9.2 Offerings

Offerings create a management bridge between town resources and divine performances.

Examples:

| Offering | Associated Gods | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Laurel | Apollo, Athena | Better cue preview or planning bonus |
| Honey Cake | Demeter, Artemis, Persephone | Gentle Favor increase, family request bonus |
| Wine | Dionysus, Aphrodite | Audience excitement, risk multiplier |
| Olive Oil | Athena, Hera, Apollo | Formal rite bonus |
| Sea Shell | Poseidon, Aphrodite | Harbor or beauty cue bonus |
| Pomegranate | Hades, Persephone, Hera | Memory or vow bonus |
| Incense | All gods | Base invocation quality |
| Votive Figure | Specific god | Large Favor boost, expensive |

### 9.3 Festival Donations

To add a Moonlighter-like management layer, public rites can include donation and attendance settings.

Before a public festival, the player sets:

- Free entry.
- Suggested donation.
- High patron seating.
- Foreign guest invitation.
- Citizen-only ceremony.

Audience response depends on:

- Town Hope.
- Reputation.
- Venue quality.
- Performance score.
- Donation fairness.
- Matching the chosen god to current town needs.

Potential outcomes:

- High donations but lower Hope if citizens feel excluded.
- Lower Coin but high Hope from free public rites.
- Rare gifts from elite patrons.
- Larger chorus participation.
- Higher risk of unrest if the event fails.

### 9.4 Market Demand

The agora changes daily.

Demand can rise or fall for:

- Grain.
- Wine.
- Incense.
- Flowers.
- Cloth.
- Marble.
- Fish.
- Laurel.
- Imported dyes.

Hermes and Athena can improve market information. Dionysus festivals raise wine demand. Demeter blessings increase grain supply. Poseidon storms disrupt imports.

### 9.5 Commissions

Commissions are daily or weekly tasks that combine simulation and performance.

Examples:

- "Perform an Apollo rite with no Stumbles to heal the House of Healing."
- "Host a Demeter harvest dance before Food drops below 20."
- "Earn 3 Aphrodite Favor while keeping donations free."
- "Invoke Hades and recover the missing ancestor name."
- "Use at least two Chorus cards during a public festival."
- "Finish a Poseidon rite with 10 Composure remaining."

Commissions should reward:

- Coin.
- Reputation.
- Card duplicates.
- Building materials.
- Rare offerings.
- Bond scenes.

Each building can have 3 to 5 levels.

Example: Ruined Theatre

| Level | Upgrade | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | Ruined | Private rites only |

## 11. Choreography Card System

### 11.1 Collection

The player's persistent collection is called the **Choreia Codex**.

The active deck is called the **Rite Deck**.

Rules:

- Full Rite Deck size is 20 cards.
- Tutorial days use 8, 12, and 16-card formats.
- Cards persist permanently.
- Duplicate cards can be merged.
- Cards are unlocked by gods, buildings, festivals, scouting, commissions, and story scenes.

### 11.2 Card Types

The Greek version uses four core card categories.

| Type | Beat Cost | Grace Cost | Primary Purpose |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Step | Usually 4 | 0 | Base movement, generates Grace |
| Turn | Usually 2 | 0 | Changes stance, sets up god cues |
| Gesture | 0 | 1-3 | Adds expression, Favor, multipliers, audience reaction |
| Rite | 0 | 1-3 | Builds Resolve, prepares offerings, protects Composure |

### 11.3 Card Anatomy

Every card should eventually define:

```text
Card Name
Type
Rarity
Beat Cost
Grace Cost

Ending Stance
Base Points
Favor Tags
Town Tags
Resolve Value
Slot Count
Divine Anchor

Upgrade Path

```

### 11.4 Stances

Stances define the physical and ritual relationship between the protagonist, god, and audience.

Core stances:

| Stance | Meaning | Gameplay Identity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Solo | Mortal dances alone before the altar | Flexible, safe, good for Artemis and Apollo |
| Partner | Mortal dances with divine avatar | High Favor, higher cue pressure |
| Chorus | Mortal leads townspeople in formation | Town benefits, audience rewards, Demeter and Dionysus |
| Veil | Liminal state between mortal and divine | Advanced cards, Aphrodite, Persephone, Hera |
| Underworld | Shadowed ritual stance | Hades and memory rites, high Resolve requirements |

MVP should use only Solo, Partner, and Chorus.

### 11.5 Grace

Grace is the phrase-local combo resource.

Rules:

- Step cards usually generate Grace.
- Gesture and Rite cards usually spend Grace.
- Grace resets at the end of each phrase by default.
- Outfits, gods, and buildings can grant Starting Grace.
- Some advanced cards can carry 1 Grace forward.

### 11.6 Resolve

Resolve is the support resource used to endure demanding divine cues.

Rules:

- Rite cards and some Turns build Resolve.
- Resolve is phrase-local by default.
- Storm, Underworld, and vow cues require Resolve.
- Missing a Resolve check can cause a Falter.

### 11.7 Composure

Composure is the nightly mistake buffer.

Rules:

- The player starts each performance with a set amount of Composure.
- Falters reduce Composure.
- Reaching 0 Composure can end the rite early.
- Ending early still gives partial rewards.
- No cards or buildings are lost.

### 11.8 Divine Cues

Divine Cues are the Greek equivalent of lead patterns. They represent what the invoked god demands, invites, or tests during the phrase.

Examples:

| Cue | God | Timing | Player Response | Success | Miss |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Golden Cadence | Apollo | Steps 5-8 | Attach a Gesture to a Step | Favor and Health bonus | Reduced Favor |
| Moonlit Distance | Artemis | Steps 1-4 | Stay Solo | Safety bonus | No bonus |
| Thunder Oath | Zeus | Step 8 | Have 20 Resolve | Major Favor | Falter, lose Composure |
| Sea Surge | Poseidon | Steps 5-8 | Play a Turn into Partner or Chorus | Coin and fish bonus | Break multiplier |
| Pomegranate Silence | Hades | Step 4 | Play a Pause or Rite card | Recover Memory | Hope penalty if missed |
| Festival Frenzy | Dionysus | Any | Chain 3 card types | Huge multiplier | Random discard |

## 16. Narrative Design

### 16.1 Premise

Heliara's old covenant with the gods has been broken. The town once promised yearly dances to honor the divine powers that sustained its water, harvest, trade, memory, and laws. Over generations, the rites became shallow festivals, then political tools, then forgotten customs.

When the Waning begins, ordinary prayers fail. The protagonist discovers the original choreography carved beneath the ruined theatre. Her first dance reaches Apollo, who reveals that the town can still be saved, but not by begging. Heliara must remember how to meet the gods through action, beauty, discipline, and communal care.

### 16.2 Protagonist

The protagonist should be:

- Mortal.
- Skilled but not famous at the start.
- Responsible for both art and civic survival.
- Capable of challenging gods.
- Emotionally perceptive.
- Strong enough to lead a town without becoming a chosen-one cliche.

Possible backgrounds:

- Former temple dancer returning home.
- Daughter of a musician and a civic magistrate.
- Orphan raised by the theatre chorus.
- Apprentice healer who learned rhythm as medicine.
- Fisherman's daughter trained in festival dances.

Recommended canon:

The protagonist is a local dancer named by the player. She left Heliara after the theatre closed, then returned when her younger sibling wrote that the springs had gone dry.

### 16.3 Supporting Mortal Cast

The town needs recurring mortal characters so the simulation layer has emotional weight.

Suggested cast:

| Character | Role | Function |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ione | Young chorus student | Tutorial, Hope, emotional stakes |
| Thales | Agora steward | Market, commissions, price feedback |
| Myrrine | Healer | Health, Apollo, citizen requests |
| Dymas | Harbor captain | Poseidon, trade routes, storm warnings |
| Kleon | Skeptical councilor | Civic politics, unrest, hard choices |
| Niko | Artisan apprentice | outfits, card upgrades, materials |
| The Old Singer | Former ritual leader | lore, memory, Hades route |

### 16.4 Act Structure

#### Act 1: The First Answer

- Heliara is in decline.
- The protagonist discovers the ancient theatre markings.
- Apollo answers the first dance.
- The player restores the Temple of Apollo and Fountain Plaza.
- Basic Step, Gesture, Grace, Composure, and Favor systems unlock.

#### Act 2: The Town Gathers

- The Agora, Granary, and Theatre reopen.
- Demeter, Artemis, Aphrodite, Hermes, and Athena become available.
- Public festivals and audience management unlock.
- The player learns that different gods disagree about how Heliara should be saved.

#### Act 3: The Forgotten Debt

- Hades and Persephone reveal that Heliara's covenant was broken by a hidden historical betrayal.
- Memory Loss becomes a major crisis track.
- Ancestor rites and Underworld stance unlock.
- The player must decide whether to reveal the truth publicly.

#### Act 4: Olympus Watches

- Zeus and Hera demand formal reckoning.
- Storm Season begins.
- The town must prove it can govern itself while honoring the divine.
- Major festivals combine multiple gods and civic systems.

#### Act 5: The New Covenant

- The player chooses Heliara's future relationship with the gods.
- Possible endings:
  - Devout City: strong divine patronage, high Piety.
  - Free City: mortal-led civic independence, high Culture and Safety.
  - Festival City: high Culture, trade, and romance routes.
  - Balanced Covenant: gods and mortals share obligations.
  - Underworld Truth: memory and justice prioritized over prosperity.

## 19. Scoring and Rewards

### 19.1 Phrase Score

Simple formula:

```text
Phrase Score = Card Points x Connection Multiplier x Audience Multiplier
```

Connection Multiplier comes from matching divine cues.

Audience Multiplier applies mainly during public festivals.

### 19.2 Favor

```text
Favor = Floor(Performance Score / Favor Divisor) + Cue Bonuses + Offering Bonus
```

Favor type depends on invoked god and card tags.

### 19.3 Town Rewards

Town rewards depend on rite type.

Examples:

- Apollo rites improve Health or Culture.
- Demeter rites improve Food.
- Artemis rites improve Safety.
- Aphrodite rites improve Hope and social reputation.
- Hermes rites improve Coin and market access.
- Hades rites reduce Memory Loss.

### 19.4 Audience Rewards

Public festival rewards:

- Coin from donations.
- Hope from citizen attendance.
- Reputation from guests.
- Chorus experience.
- Requests unlocked.
- Rare gifts from patrons.

## 20. Failure Model

Failure should produce pressure without wiping progress.

### 20.1 Falter

A Falter occurs when:

- The player misses a dangerous divine cue.
- The player lacks required Resolve.
- The player enters the wrong stance for a cue.
- A vow card objective is broken.
- A high-risk festival chain collapses.

Falter effects:

- Lose Composure.
- Break multiplier.
- Reduce Favor.
- Lower audience reaction.
- Raise a crisis track.
- Alter the cinematic animation.

### 20.2 Rite Ends Early

If Composure reaches 0, the rite can end early.

The player still receives:

- Partial Favor.
- Partial audience rewards.
- Any completed request progress.
- No loss of owned cards.
- No reset of buildings.

Possible consequences:

- Tomorrow's offerings cost more.
- Audience Hope drops.
- Patron mood becomes Testing or Disappointed.
- A crisis track rises.

### 20.3 Public Failure

Public failure should matter more than private failure.

Private shrine rite failure:

- Lower Favor.
- Lower Bond progress.
- Small Composure consequence.

Public festival failure:

- Lower Hope.
- Lower donations.
- Possible unrest.
- Reputation damage.

This creates a risk choice before the night performance.

## 27. Theme Comparison Notes

This Greek mythology version is likely more immediately legible than the vampire version because players understand gods, blessings, temples, towns, and festivals quickly.

Strengths:

- Broader cast.
- Stronger town-management fit.
- Easier expansion through gods and regions.
- Natural audience, festival, offering, and resource systems.
- More obvious public stakes.
- Strong potential for multiple routes and patron strategies.

Weaknesses:

- Less intimate than the vampire ballroom concept.
- Higher scope because every god implies content.
- More direct comparison to other Greek mythology games.
- Harder to make one romantic relationship feel central.
- Risk of becoming generic if the dance/town angle is not foregrounded.

The Greek version should be chosen if the desired game is:

- A town-saving simulation.
- A multi-patron relationship game.
- A festival-management deckbuilder.
- A broader ensemble story.

The vampire version should remain preferred if the desired game is:

- A focused gothic romance.
- A one-partner dance fantasy.
- A manor restoration sim.
- A tighter mobile MVP.

## 28. Open Design Questions

1. Is Apollo the main romantic route, or only the first patron?
2. Are all gods bondable, or only some?
3. Should the protagonist be allowed to reject divine patronage and pursue a mortal-led ending?
4. How many town stats are necessary before the simulation becomes too busy?
5. Should public festivals happen every night, or only on chosen nights?
6. Should the game include direct romance with gods, or frame most divine relationships as patronage, rivalry, and trust?
7. Should Hades and Persephone be mid-game reveals or visible from the start?
8. Should the dance system remain 8-step for readability, or introduce odd meters later?
9. Should Zeus be a route, an authority figure, or mainly a crisis patron?
10. How much historical Greek detail should be used versus mythic fantasy accessibility?
11. Should citizen deaths or departures be possible, or is that too harsh for the target experience?
12. How should the game avoid making offerings feel like simple bribery?
13. Can the town-management layer be fun in 5-10 minute sessions?
14. How many gods are realistic for MVP, first release, and live expansion?
